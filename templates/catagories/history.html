In 1834 General Mariano Vallejo divided up land north of San Francisco into land grants sanctioned by the Mexican government. Old Vine Farms sits on a piece of the original El Molino Mexican Land Grant. Molino, Spanish for mill, refers to the oldest powered commercial sawmill in California.

In the late 19th century, Italian immigrants came to the North Bay having entered through the port of San Francisco via what we now call The Ferry Building. Giuseppe Martinelli ( age 19 ) and Luisa Vellutini ( age 16 ) eloped from their small village in Tuscany, and made their way to the Russian River Valley. The roads that intersect at the corner of Old Vine Farms still bear their names to this day.

The climate of the Russian River Valley proved excellent for growing grapes. Giuseppe and Luisa picked hops, made charcoal and cut timber, in an effort to generate enough money to buy property. On Sundays they would bike and hike throughout the valley looking for land. In 1902 they were able to purchase a piece of the original El Molino Mexican Land grant for $2,625.00. They brought deep root stock vines from Tuscany and were able to start their dry farm vineyard by the beautiful Green Valley River.

They soon had a thriving family, three boys and one girl: Alfredo, Dominic, Etta and Leno. Later, Luisa sent a request to her Tuscan village for two mail order brides. Steerage tickets were procured and the two women set out from Italy by ship for Northern California. Two sisters ( Pia and Natalina ) arrived safely to San Francisco and eventually married Alfredo and Dominic. Two brothers married two sisters.

Years later when Luisa and Giuseppe died, the property was divided amongst the siblings. The house at Old Vine Farms was built in 1940 by Dominic and Pia, but the exact date the barn was built is unknown. With Luisa and Giuseppe gone, Pia became the “hearth” of the family. An excellent cook, gardener and homemaker, Pia planted fruit trees and olives, figs and walnuts. To this day Old Vine Farms continues to harvest and preserve the fruits and nuts from these same trees. The heritage grapes that Giuseppe and Luisa envisioned flourishing, are still lovingly tended by hand and protected. The restoration of the farm has been a continuing labor of love for the last ten years, keeping to the original footprint of the farm, and preserving the unique beauty of the Green Valley River and its West County history.
