"To restore a farm is to restore life itself."
It frightened me, when I first laid eyes on the the farm.

Like a once grand lady, now down-at-heel, faded and forgotten, a battered kind of beauty. There was a melancholy feeling to everything. And there was the quiet.

The stark silhouette of the locust tree, the scent of the big bay, the woodpecker leaving its mark on the bark of the black walnut trees. The overgrown blackberry vines, lush with fruit and bees, left to the birds all these fallow years. The silence. In the absence of sound, even the wings of a hummingbird can be heard.

California quail cooed under the the hedgerows, foraging alongside the jackrabbits and deer. The fences untended and un-mended. Italian dry-farm grapes brought from Italy in 1886, gnarled and wild, survivors of frost and flood. Wise. I immediately whispered “grandparents” and reached out to touch one.

The stars at night were so close, so clear. And bit by bit, time and place, spirit and story were revealed. All the secrets. The fruit ripened, the ground gave back, the feral tamed, and I was glad I had said yes to all this that once frightened me. There are times when a place calls to you, as if somewhere in your DNA you recognize but can’t quite name your own kin. This is that story, This is the story of

The Farm.
